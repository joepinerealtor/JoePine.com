First Steps Toward Buying

If you are not sure where to begin, you are in the right place.

This workbook is for someone who wants to buy a home but is not quite sure they are ready yet. You do not need perfect credit or every answer today, just a clearer next step.

Move through the steps one at a time and use this as a calm place to understand what matters now, what can wait, and how to build a real plan before you talk with a lender.

Step 4

Now turn that picture into a plan.

The first few steps are there to help you see why homeownership can matter. This is where the workbook shifts from comparison into what to work on next.

You do not need to be fully ready today

Most buyers do not begin with perfect credit, perfect timing, or every dollar in place. They begin by understanding what will move them forward.

Small improvements can change the timing

Credit cleanup, lower monthly debt, and better cash planning can make buying feel a lot more possible than it did a few months earlier.

This is where the workbook gets practical

From here, the goal is simple: understand what to work on so you can move toward preapproval with more confidence and less guesswork.

What to do next

  • Keep the homeowner goal in view, but do not panic if you are not ready this second.
  • Use the next steps to understand credit, monthly debt, and assistance options in the state where you want to buy.
  • Focus on building a realistic path instead of trying to solve everything at once.

Joe would be happy to sit down with you and help turn this into a real plan. The goal is not pressure. The goal is helping you understand what would move you from renter to homeowner in a way that actually makes sense for your life.

Step 5

Know what is actually on your credit.

Most people do not need to guess their way into buying a home. Start by seeing the real picture: payment history, credit-card balances, collections, and monthly debt.

Pull the real reports

Start with the actual bureau data, not just a score from a phone app.

Find what is fixable

Late payments, high card balances, and unresolved collections usually matter more than people think.

Get specific

The goal is not "better credit." The goal is knowing which move helps your buying path most.

What to do next

  • Pull your credit reports and make sure the accounts, balances, and late payments actually look right.
  • List every monthly debt payment, including cars, student loans, credit cards, and personal loans.
  • Flag anything that looks wrong, duplicate, or too old before you ever start a preapproval conversation.

Joe can help you turn this into an actual plan. Sometimes the next best move is credit cleanup. Sometimes it is just understanding payment comfort before you talk to a lender.

Step 6

You do not need perfect credit to buy a home.

Think of these as common starting points, not promises. Lenders can add their own overlays, but this is still a useful way to understand which credit range may be worth exploring first.

Many conventional paths use 620+ as a practical starting point. That does not mean every 620 file works, but it is a common benchmark.

Common starting point Often 620+
What else matters Debt load, cash reserves, and clean recent history
A 620 score with manageable debt and stable cash can be more workable than a higher score with heavy monthly obligations.

FHA is often where people start when they need more flexibility. A common starting point is 580+ for 3.5% down, while 500-579 may still be possible with 10% down.

Common starting point 580+ for 3.5% down
Lower-score path 500-579 may mean 10% down
Many lenders still add stricter rules on top of FHA. That is why talking with an actual lender matters after you know which credit range you are likely exploring.

RI Housing can be a strong path for first-time buyers, especially when assistance is part of the plan. Many RI Housing help paths start around 620, while some targeted help paths can be closer to 660.

Common starting point Many help paths start near 620
Know this too Education or counseling can be part of the path
RI Housing help can be powerful, but it still works best when the monthly payment and debt picture are already under control.

MA Housing, through MassHousing programs, can also be a strong path, but minimums vary by loan, property type, and loan-to-value. Many scenarios land in the mid-600s and can run higher.

Common starting point Often mid-600s, sometimes higher
Know this too Program, property, and lender details matter
MassHousing can be a great fit, but this is the kind of program where an approved lender helps you figure out which exact path applies.

What to do next

  • Figure out which credit starting point looks most like you right now.
  • Assume the lender may still be stricter than the headline minimum.
  • Use this step to decide whether your next move is credit cleanup, cash cleanup, or a lender conversation.

Step 7

Lower monthly pressure before you add a mortgage.

A lot of buyers do not need a miracle. They need a cleaner monthly picture. Lower debt and lower recurring expenses can do more for buying power than people expect.

Pay down card balances

Keeping card balances lower helps more than many buyers realize. Lower utilization can support a stronger score and a cleaner monthly picture.

Avoid new monthly payments

Cars, personal loans, furniture financing, and store cards can all shrink what a lender is willing to approve.

Tighten recurring expenses

If your monthly budget already feels stretched, it is worth going line by line before you add homeownership costs on top.

What to do next

  • Try to keep credit-card balances low relative to the limit. Staying under about 15-20% utilization is usually healthier for a score, while balances over about 50% often start hurting more.
  • Avoid new monthly payments if buying is a real short-term goal.
  • Go through your monthly expenses and subscriptions. Car insurance, phone bills, streaming, memberships, and other recurring charges are all worth revisiting.
  • If your phone is paid off, it may be worth comparing lower-cost carriers. Some plans can still offer unlimited data in roughly the $15-$25 per line range, depending on the carrier and plan details.
  • Get clear on the monthly payment that actually feels comfortable in your life, not just what a lender might approve.

Step 8

Understand the first-time-buyer assistance that may be available.

These programs can help with down payment, closing costs, or mortgage structure, but they still work best when the payment, debt, and cash side already make sense. Think of assistance as part of the plan, not the whole plan.

RI Housing

RI Housing has a few different first-time-buyer paths, and the right one usually depends on your credit, income, cash-to-close needs, and whether you qualify for a targeted program.

  • Extra Assistance: down payment and closing-cost help, often up to 6% of the price or $20,000, paired with a RIHousing first mortgage.
  • 15kDPA: $15,000 in zero-percent assistance for down payment and/or closing costs.
  • FirstGenHomeRI: $25,000 in down payment and/or closing-cost help for qualifying first-generation buyers.
  • RI REALTORS Homebuyer Grant: limited-time grant help that may pair with RIHousing assistance paths when available.
  • Homebuyer education is often part of the process.

MA Housing

MassHousing is more of a mortgage-and-benefits platform than a single assistance program. Buyers in Massachusetts may be looking at a MassHousing mortgage paired with other benefits built around it.

  • MassHousing Down Payment Assistance: up to $30,000, depending on eligibility and lender determination.
  • MassHousing mortgage loans: affordable mortgage options meant to pair with MassHousing benefits.
  • MIPlus: job-loss mortgage payment protection on MassHousing-insured loans at no added cost.
  • Veteran and service-member closing credit: a $2,500 closing-cost credit for eligible military households.
  • Program details vary by income, property type, approved lender, and exact loan structure.

What to do next

  • Match the housing agency to the state where you are actually buying: RI Housing for Rhode Island, MassHousing for Massachusetts.
  • Then narrow the question to which program path fits your starting point, budget, and cash-to-close needs.
  • Remember that assistance can help, but it does not replace qualifying for the payment.

Step 9

Be ready for the early cash costs of getting under contract.

Even with good assistance options, most buyers still need a little liquid cash ready for the process itself. The goal here is not panic. The goal is knowing which costs tend to hit before closing so they do not catch you off guard.

Keep a small cushion in the bank

Many buyers feel more stable with roughly $3,000-$5,000 saved for the early parts of the process. That can help cover the inspection, appraisal, and earnest money timing without making every next step feel stressful.

Inspection and appraisal are usually out-of-pocket

A home inspection is your chance to have a licensed inspector look at the home's condition, systems, and visible issues before you move forward. It often lands around $400-$650 and is usually paid when you book it. An appraisal is the lender's value check on the home to make sure it supports the price and loan amount, and it often lands around $500-$700 before closing. Those are normal buying costs, even when assistance is helping elsewhere.

EMD shows the seller you are serious

The earnest money deposit, or EMD, is the good-faith money that usually goes in after an offer is accepted to show the seller you are serious. A common planning range is around 0.5%-2% of the purchase price, and for many first-time-buyer offers that may look more like roughly $1,500-$3,500. It is usually held in escrow, then credited toward your down payment and/or closing costs at closing instead of being an extra charge on top.

What to do next

  • Try to keep roughly $3,000-$5,000 liquid if you can, so a $400-$650 inspection, a $500-$700 appraisal, and deposit timing do not feel overwhelming.
  • Remember that the inspection is about the home's condition, the appraisal is the lender's value check, and the EMD is your good-faith deposit after an offer is accepted.
  • Ask what earnest money range is common in the price range where you plan to buy, but expect many offers to land somewhere around $1,500-$3,500 depending on the home and the competition.
  • Remember that how an EMD is protected or returned depends on the contract, contingencies, and deadlines, so it is worth understanding before you write an offer.

Step 10

Build the file that gets you preapproval-ready.

Even when the credit is workable, buyers often lose momentum because the paperwork is scattered. Pulling it together early makes the lender conversation a lot easier.

Common documents

  • Recent pay stubs
  • W-2s and, when needed, tax returns
  • Recent bank statements
  • Photo ID
  • Employment history and housing history

Do not make this harder

  • Do not open new credit right before preapproval.
  • Do not move money around without being able to explain it.
  • Do not assume cash-to-close will figure itself out later.

What to do next

  • Pull your income documents together before you need them.
  • Get clear on which cash is truly available for down payment, closing costs, reserves, or gift funds.
  • Write down the questions you want answered before you apply so the lender conversation is focused.
